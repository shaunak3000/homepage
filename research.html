Research

My research sits where finance, operations, and risk converge — because the most consequential business decisions rarely fall neatly into one discipline. From credit derivatives and portfolio optimization, to supply chain disruption and circular markets: the common thread is quantitative rigor applied to problems with real stakes. Occasionally, that same rigor gets pointed at sport — and finds equally interesting problems.

My research work in Mathematical Finance focuses on Credit Risk, Pricing and Portfolio Optimization.

Portfolio Optimization with Credit Default Swaps
Portfolio OptimizationCredit Default SwapsPhD Dissertation

Two linked problems: pricing a CDS and optimizing a portfolio across a CDS, the underlying firm's stock, and a money market account. The CDS is priced using a doubly stochastic process driven by the firm's stock price and random jumps, in a regime-switching market. Optimal portfolio strategies are derived explicitly via a Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman framework for an investor with logarithmic utility.

PhD Dissertation, Purdue University.

Valuing Switching Options
Real OptionsMoving BoundaryJournal of Economic Dynamics & Control

We develop a new computational method to value switching options using a moving boundary approach, solved across two regimes for a finite time horizon. The methodology applies to power plant operations — where a generator is switched on or off depending on fuel costs and electricity revenues — and to near-shoring/offshoring decisions where a firm switches production between facilities based on costs and trade barriers.

Assigning Sovereign Credit Ratings from CDS Spreads
Sovereign RiskCredit RatingsCDS

We develop a model that uses CDS spreads on sovereign debt to extract implied default rates, then applies regression to predict future default rates and assign credit ratings. Our model outperforms those used by the Big-3 ratings agencies, providing accurate ratings of EU sovereign debt months before Moody's, S&P, and Fitch adjust theirs.

Basel III: Economic Capital for Strategic Risk
Basel IIIEconomic CapitalBanking Regulation

Basel III mandates capital reserves for strategic risk, yet no agreed measurable definition exists. We propose a practitioner-verified definition and a simulation-based framework to estimate economic capital — using the cost of equity as the profitability threshold. Validated against ABN AMRO's actual capital outlay, the framework is simple, generalizable, and immediately implementable.

This research examines how financial decisions and external shocks — trade policies, tariffs, and operational crises — propagate through supply networks, combining operations models with financial market analysis.

Working Capital Management in Supply Networks
Trade CreditWorking CapitalSupply NetworksUnder Review · Journal of Operations Management

Aggressive trade credit policies may boost a firm's own profitability but harms its supply chain partners. We show that firms adopting industry-standard, collaborative trade policies increase profitability across the entire supply chain — making the case for inclusive, network-aware financial strategy.

Global Sourcing under Tariffs
Global SourcingTariffsTime Series Clustering

Not all supply chains respond to trade shocks the same way. Using the 2018–2019 U.S.–China tariffs as a natural experiment, we uncover seven distinct global sourcing responses through time series clustering. The key differentiator: firms with stronger financials and higher growth potential weathered the disruption far better — revealing that a supply chain's resilience is ultimately a reflection of the firm behind it.

Disruption Propagation through Complex Supply Networks
Disruption ManagementEvent StudySupply Network RiskUnder Review · Journal of Operations Management

Using the Boeing 737MAX crisis as a natural experiment, we trace how a single firm's operational failure ripples through tier-1 and tier-2 suppliers and downstream customers via abnormal stock returns. We find that markets respond not only to the severity of a disruption but to what it signals about operational health — and document cross-competitor spillovers into Airbus's supply network among shared suppliers.

This research spans two interconnected themes: the economics of circular markets for refurbished products, and the governance of sustainability across supply chains. Together they examine how firms can extract more value from existing goods while managing their broader environmental and social footprint.

The Goldilocks Problem of Refurbished Launches
Product LaunchPricingCircular EconomyUnder Review · Flexible Systems and Manufacturing

Launch a refurbished model too early and it cannibalizes new product sales; too late and newer versions have already eroded demand. We determine the optimal launch time, price, and quantity — navigating the tension between cannibalization and obsolescence to maximize value from the refurbished market.

Sustainability Starts Upstream
Supplier SustainabilityLiterature ReviewSupply Chain Management

A firm's supply chain typically has a larger environmental and social footprint than its own operations — yet most firms focus sustainability efforts inward. We review the fragmented landscape of supplier sustainability management (SSM) research across disciplines, proposing the MMAIG framework (Motivation–Measure–Analyze–Improve–Govern) to map the field and chart a path forward. The diagnosis: research is rich in measurement, thin on improvement. The prescription: collaborative, data-driven, and trust-based approaches that align incentives across the entire chain.
